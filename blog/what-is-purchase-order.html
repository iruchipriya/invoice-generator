What Is a Purchase Order and How Does It Relate to an Invoice?

By InvoiceGen Team · Last updated: March 23, 2026 • 11 min read

Key Takeaways

  • A purchase order (PO) is a buyer's formal authorization to purchase goods or services at agreed-upon terms
  • The procurement cycle follows a clear sequence: PO issued, goods or services delivered, invoice sent, payment made
  • Large companies use 3-way matching (PO, delivery receipt, and invoice) to prevent fraud and overpayment
  • Freelancers working with corporate clients must understand PO workflows to avoid invoice rejections and payment delays

If you have ever invoiced a large company and been told "we need a PO number before we can process this," you know the confusion that purchase orders can cause. You did the work, you sent the invoice, and now payment is stuck because of a document you may never have heard of before. For freelancers and small business owners who are used to informal agreements and straightforward invoicing, the purchase order system can feel like unnecessary bureaucracy.

But purchase orders exist for good reasons, and understanding how they work will make you a more effective vendor. This guide explains what a purchase order is, how it differs from an invoice, how the two documents work together in the procurement cycle, and what you need to know as a freelancer to navigate PO-based workflows without delays.

What Is a Purchase Order?

A purchase order is a formal document issued by a buyer to a seller that authorizes a purchase. It specifies exactly what the buyer wants to purchase, the quantity, the agreed price, delivery terms, and payment terms. Once the seller accepts a purchase order, it becomes a legally binding contract between the two parties.

Think of a purchase order as the buyer saying, in writing, "We commit to buying these specific items or services at this specific price, and we authorize you to deliver them." It is the buyer's commitment, not the seller's request. This distinction is important: the PO comes from the client, while the invoice comes from you.

A typical purchase order contains the following information:

  • PO number — a unique identifier assigned by the buyer's system
  • Buyer details — company name, billing address, and contact person
  • Seller details — your business name and address
  • Description of goods or services — what is being purchased
  • Quantities and unit prices — how much of each item and at what rate
  • Total amount — the authorized spending amount
  • Delivery date or timeline — when the goods or services are expected
  • Payment terms — Net 30, Net 60, or other agreed terms
  • Shipping and delivery instructions — where and how to deliver
Key point: A purchase order is not an invoice. The PO is created by the buyer before any work is done. The invoice is created by the seller after the work is completed. They are two different documents that reference each other.

Purchase Order vs. Invoice: A Direct Comparison

The confusion between purchase orders and invoices is understandable because they contain similar information — descriptions, quantities, prices, and totals. However, they serve fundamentally different purposes and are created by different parties at different stages of the transaction.

Attribute Purchase Order Invoice
Created by Buyer (client) Seller (you, the vendor)
Purpose Authorizes a purchase before work begins Requests payment after work is delivered
When issued Before goods or services are delivered After goods or services are delivered
Legal function Buyer's commitment to pay for specified goods/services Seller's formal demand for payment owed
Unique identifier PO number (assigned by buyer) Invoice number (assigned by seller)
Triggers Delivery of goods or start of services Payment processing by the buyer
Typical recipients Vendor's sales or account management team Client's accounts payable department

The simplest way to remember the difference: a purchase order says "please deliver this," and an invoice says "please pay for this." They are two halves of the same transaction. For a related comparison, see our article on the difference between an invoice and a bill.

The Procurement Cycle: How POs and Invoices Work Together

In a formal procurement process, purchase orders and invoices are two steps in a structured sequence. Understanding this full cycle helps you see where your invoice fits and why it matters that everything aligns perfectly.

Step 1: The buyer creates a purchase order

The client's procurement team or hiring manager creates a PO in their accounting system. This document is reviewed and approved internally, often by a budget holder or department head. The PO authorizes spending a specific amount on specific goods or services from a specific vendor. Once approved, the PO is sent to the vendor.

Step 2: The seller accepts the purchase order

You receive the PO, review the details — scope, quantities, prices, timeline — and either accept it or negotiate changes. Accepting the PO means you are agreeing to deliver what is specified at the stated price. At this point, you have a binding agreement and can begin work.

Step 3: Goods or services are delivered

You complete the work or deliver the goods as specified in the PO. For physical goods, a delivery receipt or goods received note is typically created by the buyer to confirm that the items arrived in the correct quantity and condition. For services, this might be a project completion sign-off or a timesheet approval.

Step 4: The seller sends an invoice

After delivery, you create an invoice that references the PO number. The invoice details should match the PO — same descriptions, same quantities, same prices. Use our freelance invoice checklist to make sure every required field is included. Any discrepancy between the PO and the invoice will cause delays because the buyer's accounts payable team will flag it for review.

Step 5: The buyer processes payment

The buyer's accounts payable team receives your invoice, matches it against the original PO and the delivery confirmation, and processes payment according to the agreed terms. If everything matches, payment is straightforward. If there are discrepancies, the invoice goes into a dispute queue, and you will need to resolve the differences before getting paid.

Tip: Always reference the PO number prominently on your invoice — typically in a dedicated "PO Number" field near the top. Accounts payable teams process hundreds of invoices and they need to match yours to the right PO instantly. An invoice without a PO number will often be returned unpaid.

Why Large Companies Require Purchase Orders

If you work primarily with small businesses or individual clients, you may wonder why large companies insist on this seemingly rigid process. There are several compelling reasons that become clear once you understand the scale at which these organizations operate.

Budget control. A company with hundreds of employees and dozens of departments needs a systematic way to track who is spending what. Purchase orders create an authorization trail — every dollar spent must be pre-approved through a PO, which means no one can commit company funds without proper authorization. This prevents rogue spending and ensures departments stay within budget.

Fraud prevention. Without purchase orders, a dishonest employee could create fake invoices from fictitious vendors and approve them for payment. The PO system requires a separate authorization step before any invoice can be paid, making it significantly harder to process fraudulent payments.

Audit trail. Purchase orders create a complete paper trail from the initial purchase decision through delivery to final payment. Auditors can trace every transaction from end to end, verify that goods and services were actually received, and confirm that prices paid match what was agreed upon.

Cash flow forecasting. Outstanding POs represent committed future spending. A company's finance team can look at all open POs to forecast upcoming cash outflows, which is essential for managing working capital and liquidity.

How Freelancers Handle PO-to-Invoice Workflows

As a freelancer, you will encounter PO requirements most often when working with mid-size and large companies, government agencies, and educational institutions. Here is how to handle the workflow smoothly.

Ask about PO requirements during onboarding

Before you start work, ask your client: "Does your company require a purchase order for this engagement?" If the answer is yes, do not begin work until you have the PO in hand. Starting work without a PO is risky — if the PO never materializes, you may have no formal authorization to bill for your work, and the client's accounts payable team may refuse to process your invoice.

Match your invoice to the PO exactly

When you create your invoice, ensure that the descriptions, quantities, and amounts match the PO precisely. If the PO says "Website copywriting — 10 pages — $200/page — $2,000" then your invoice line item should say exactly the same thing. Even minor discrepancies in wording can trigger a mismatch flag in automated systems and delay payment.

Warning: If the scope of work changes after the PO is issued, do not simply invoice for the new amount. Ask the client to issue a revised PO or a supplementary PO that covers the additional work. Invoicing above the PO amount will almost certainly result in your invoice being rejected.

Track PO numbers in your records

Maintain a simple spreadsheet or record that links each client's PO number to your corresponding invoice number. This makes it easy to reference the correct PO when creating invoices and helps you respond quickly when a client asks about a specific transaction.

3-Way Matching: PO, Receipt, and Invoice

Three-way matching is the quality control process that many large organizations use to verify that a payment is legitimate before releasing funds. It involves comparing three documents to confirm they all agree.

  1. Purchase order — confirms what was authorized to be purchased and at what price
  2. Goods received note or delivery receipt — confirms that the goods or services were actually received in the correct quantity and quality
  3. Invoice — confirms what the vendor is charging for the delivery

If all three documents match — same items, same quantities, same prices — the invoice is approved for payment automatically. If there is a discrepancy between any of the three, the invoice is flagged for manual review. Common mismatches include quantity differences (you shipped 95 units but the PO said 100), price differences (your invoice rate differs from the PO rate), or description differences (the PO says "consulting" but the invoice says "advisory services").

Document Created By What It Confirms
Purchase Order Buyer What was authorized to be purchased and the agreed price
Goods Received Note Buyer (receiving dept.) What was actually delivered and its condition
Invoice Seller What the seller is charging and the total amount due

For freelancers, the "goods received note" equivalent is often a project sign-off, an approved timesheet, or a delivery confirmation email. Make sure you always get written confirmation that your work has been accepted before sending your invoice. Understanding the difference between an invoice and a receipt also helps you keep the right documents at each stage. This confirmation is the second leg of the 3-way match, and without it, your invoice may sit in limbo.

Note: Some organizations use 2-way matching (PO and invoice only) for low-value purchases or trusted vendors. Others use 4-way matching that adds a quality inspection step. The principle is the same — every document in the chain must agree before payment is released.

When Purchase Orders Are Not Needed

Not every business transaction requires a purchase order. Understanding when POs are and are not expected will save you from creating unnecessary paperwork and help you set the right expectations with each client.

  • Small businesses and startups. Companies with fewer than 50 employees rarely use formal PO systems. Purchases are typically approved informally via email or verbal agreement, and invoices are paid directly without a matching process.
  • One-off small purchases. Many companies set a threshold below which POs are not required — often $500 to $1,000. If your project falls below this threshold, you may be able to invoice directly.
  • Individual clients. When your client is a person rather than a company, purchase orders are almost never used. A written agreement or email confirmation of the project scope and price is sufficient.
  • Recurring retainer arrangements. Some companies issue a single blanket PO to cover a full year of retainer services. You invoice monthly against this single PO, referencing the same PO number each time, until the authorized amount is exhausted.
  • Emergency purchases. In urgent situations, some organizations allow purchases without a PO, with a retroactive PO created afterward to maintain the audit trail. This is the exception, not the rule.
Tip: When in doubt, ask. A simple question during the project kickoff — "Will I need to reference a purchase order number on my invoices?" — can prevent weeks of payment delay later. It also signals to the client that you understand professional procurement processes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I start work before receiving a purchase order?

It is risky. Without a PO, you have no formal authorization from the client's organization to perform the work, which means their accounts payable team may refuse to process your invoice. If a client asks you to start immediately and says the PO is coming, get that promise in writing from someone with budget authority. Ideally, wait for the actual PO before beginning work, especially for large projects or new client relationships.

What happens if my invoice amount exceeds the PO amount?

The invoice will almost certainly be rejected or flagged for manual review. Accounts payable teams are typically not authorized to pay more than the PO amount without additional approval. If the scope of work expanded beyond the original PO, contact your client and request a revised PO or a supplementary PO before sending your invoice. Never assume the client will simply approve the overage.

Do freelancers need to issue purchase orders to their own suppliers?

Most freelancers do not need to issue formal POs for their own purchases. PO systems make sense when you have multiple people making purchasing decisions and you need centralized control. As a solo freelancer, your own approval is sufficient. However, if your freelance business grows to include subcontractors or employees, implementing a simple PO process can help you track committed spending and maintain clean financial records.

Is a purchase order legally binding?

Yes. Once a seller accepts a purchase order, it becomes a legally binding contract. The buyer is committed to paying the stated price for the specified goods or services, and the seller is committed to delivering them. This is why it is important to review PO details carefully before accepting. If the terms, quantities, or prices are incorrect, negotiate changes before acceptance rather than after delivery.

How do blanket purchase orders work for ongoing freelance engagements?

A blanket purchase order covers a defined period of time, usually six months or a year, and authorizes spending up to a specified total amount. For example, a client might issue a blanket PO for $24,000 to cover 12 months of your retainer services at $2,000 per month. You invoice monthly against this single PO number. When the PO amount is nearly exhausted, you request a new blanket PO for the next period. This arrangement reduces administrative overhead for both parties while maintaining the formal procurement trail the client requires.
